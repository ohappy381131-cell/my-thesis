# [Column] Data Is Not the Answer: Guarding Against the Catastrophe of "Automated Bias"

Autonomous driving and robotics are accelerating toward a future painted in rosy colors. Tech companies celebrate statistical efficiency that overwhelms human intuition, insisting that "the more data accumulates, the more perfect AI will become." But there is a fatal logical hole in this promise: the bias embedded in training data. An AI raised on biased data is not simply an AI with "insufficient intelligence" — it is evolving into a machine of bias that mistakes its own errors for the correct answer.

## 1. The Paradox of Learning — Two Different Errors Collapsed Into One

There are broadly two philosophies in the autonomous driving industry. Waymo uses sensor fusion, combining lidar, radar, and cameras. Tesla, by contrast, removed radar entirely starting in 2021 and adopted a "vision-only" approach, training its system on cameras alone. The reasoning goes like this: roads were designed around human vision in the first place, so it should be enough to train a system that imitates the way humans drive with their eyes. Tesla has also justified removing radar on the grounds that conflicting signals from multiple sensors could paralyze judgment rather than improve it.

This approach sounds appealing. But it actually conflates two distinct problems, and failing to distinguish them means missing exactly where and how accidents go unprevented.

**The first is the fundamental limitation of induction.** Data is only a record of the past; it cannot contain every variable that will arise in the future. No matter how vast the dataset, there is no guarantee of what a model will do when it encounters a novel combination of circumstances it has never observed — an out-of-distribution situation. This is not a matter of insufficient data; it is a structural limitation of induction as a method itself. The premise "imitating human vision will make it as safe as a human" falls within this very limitation — unlike lidar, cameras cannot directly measure three-dimensional information; they must estimate depth from two-dimensional images, and in conditions where visual information itself is degraded — fog, dust, strong glare — both the human eye and the camera that imitates it become equally vulnerable. In fact, in March 2026, the U.S. National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) escalated its investigation into Tesla's vision-only system to its final stage, citing several cases — including a fatality — in which cameras allegedly missed hazards that lidar would have detected under low-visibility conditions.

**The second is bias within the data itself.** If the training data contains distorted records regarding particular environments or subjects, the AI learns that distortion as a "statistical correct answer." Data is a mirror reflecting the discrimination and ignorance of past societies, along with the self-interested value judgments of corporations. When an AI trained on that unaltered mirror renders judgments on a complex construction site or in the middle of a city, the result is not accident "prevention" — it is the automation of a flawed standard buried in biased data.

These two problems differ both in when they arise and in how they must be solved. The limitation of induction is a problem that remains, in principle, no matter how thoroughly the data is refined. Bias, on the other hand, is a problem that can largely be caught at the pre-deployment data curation stage. The moment these two are collapsed into the simple claim that "imitating humans and gathering more data will solve it," bias goes unaddressed while the limitation of induction is over-optimistically dismissed.

## 2. Why Doesn't the Accident Rate Go Down? — Static Bias and the Feedback Loop Are Different Mechanisms

Many believe that more data will bring the accident rate down. But here too, two distinct mechanisms must be separated.

One is **static bias**. Bias inscribed into the training data from the start exists from the very moment the model trained on that data is deployed. When a biased AI encounters a situation it doesn't understand, it forces that situation into its own biased logic. Without even recognizing that it is making an error, it acts with the conviction that it is rendering the "optimal judgment."

The other is the **feedback loop of error**. This occurs after deployment. When accident data is fed back into the training pipeline, there is a risk that the very biased standard of judgment that caused the accident gets reinforced along with it. Rather than correcting the error, the system relearns from the data generated by the accident and cements that error into the essence of the system. Within this loop, the system becomes increasingly trapped inside its own biased world, and as a result, major accidents are bound to recur in blind spots we cannot see.

Static bias is a problem that must be caught through **pre-deployment audits**; the feedback loop is a problem that must be blocked through **ongoing third-party verification of the retraining pipeline**. Without this distinction, the phrase "gather more data" — which sounds like a universal solution — actually fails to target either problem precisely.

## 3. The Cunning Hidden Behind the Name "Efficiency"

The current economic structure prioritizes technological profit, placing efficiency ahead of data transparency. Whose data is it, really, that these companies provide? Passenger convenience? Corporate legal cover? Or public safety? When data whose value judgments are already biased becomes the skeleton of an algorithm, technology is no longer a fair arbiter.

One thing needs to be made clear here — the problem is not treating safety as a matter of probability in itself. All safety engineering — aviation, nuclear power — is, by nature, a matter of probability, and managing safety by a probabilistic standard, such as "the probability of a fatal accident per billion hours," is the standard approach. **The real problem is that the data feeding into that probability calculation is itself biased.** A probability calculated from biased data may look, on the surface, like a scientific and objective figure, but it may in fact be a number adjusted to represent the interests of a particular group or to cut costs. The core of the problem isn't the use of probability — it's who calculated that probability, with what data, and for what purpose.

## Conclusion — We Must Verify Human Honesty, Not Just the Machine

We must no longer be dazzled by the quantitative expansion implied in the question "how much data has it learned." The real question lies elsewhere — **if an AI renders a biased judgment, is that because the AI hid something, or because the humans who supplied the material for that judgment were already biased?**

The two problems this column has addressed — the limitation of induction and bias in the data — look like technical problems on the surface, but they ultimately converge on a single question. A camera missing a hazard in the fog is not dishonest; it has simply inherited the limitation already present in human vision. If data records a particular subject in a distorted way, that distortion was not invented by the AI — it is a direct reflection of the discrimination and ignorance of past societies, and of corporations' self-interested value judgments. **AI is a mirror of the data humans gave it.** If humans are not honest, the judgments reflected in that mirror cannot be honest either.

So demanding an abstract "ethical responsibility" is not enough. What we must actually demand, beyond the machine's judgment itself, are **concrete mechanisms for verifying the honesty of the humans who produced that judgment.**

- **Mandatory pre-deployment bias audits**: a procedure in which a third party verifies, before a model is deployed on real roads or in the field, whether data regarding particular environments or subject groups is distorted. This is not an inspection of the AI — it is an inspection of what bias the humans and organizations who supplied its data built into it.
- **Ongoing verification of the post-accident retraining pipeline**: periodic audits of how accident data is incorporated into retraining, and whether bias is being reinforced in the process. This, too, is not a question about machine malfunction — it asks whether humans are honestly handling the opportunity to correct an error.
- **Mandatory disclosure of the transparency (interpretability) behind judgments**: when an accident occurs, a legally required log that can reconstruct what the model based its judgment on at that moment, made available for disclosure. This is not a device that demands honesty from a machine — it is a device that forces the humans who built that machine to reveal their design and their data without concealment.

Technology that does not acknowledge its own bias is dangerous. But put more precisely — **it is humans who do not acknowledge their own bias, receiving that same bias back in automated form.** Automation with no capacity to correct its own errors is a catastrophe, but that incapacity does not belong to the machine — it belongs to the humans who, from the start, did not design honestly and did not handle their data honestly. Genuine technological progress begins not with the sheer volume of data, but with how honestly humans can lay bare the grounds of their own judgment.

We must demand of technology companies not the quantity of their data, but **concrete mechanisms for verifying honesty.** If we fail to do so, we will end up entrusting our lives to a machine we built to prevent accidents — a machine that, in truth, simply reflects our own dishonesty back at us.
