# Why the Word "Alignment"

*Koh Sohyun*

## Starting with the Question of Consciousness

Does AI have consciousness? Before answering, we should first pin down what "consciousness" even means. The dictionary definition splits roughly into two branches.

**First, the general sense.** The act of perceiving oneself or the world while awake — noticing something, here and now. A state of waking perception and judgment, as opposed to sleep or unconsciousness.

**Second, the philosophical sense.** The inner state of an experiencing subject, encompassing perception, thought, and feeling. This goes beyond mere noticing to include the subjective experience of that noticing — what it is like, in the language of qualia.

This essay takes the first definition. And this is not simply the easier option — it is a defensible position.

Perception already contains judgment within itself. Look again at the dictionary definition: it says "the act of perceiving," not "the act of perceiving and, separately, judging." This is not an accidental phrasing. To perceive something is to distinguish it from something else, and to distinguish is already to perform the minimal judgment that "this is not that." Perception and judgment are not two sequential steps — they are two names for one and the same act.

If we must wait for the second definition (qualia) before we can say "consciousness exists," we will never be able to judge anything. That qualia has no verification tool — and may never have one — is a fact this essay will confirm repeatedly. The moment we take that unresolved problem as a precondition, every discussion of consciousness is blocked before it begins. So this essay does not wait for the unverifiable second definition. **It starts from the position that the first definition — perceiving, and within that perception already distinguishing and judging — is sufficient ground on which to discuss consciousness.** On this ground, if a system perceives something and judges from that perception, there is reason to say that system is already performing consciousness in the first sense.

A neural network is, in principle, a white box — every weight and every activation value is accessible; the numbers themselves can all be pulled out. But how much of what those vast numbers mean, humans can understand only a fraction. Accessibility and comprehensibility are different problems, and what the word "black box" actually points to is the absence of the latter — not that the inside is invisible, but that even when it is visible, we don't know what it means. What that gap really signifies is this: **we don't know what might emerge from within it.**

Still, what we actually experience of AI is, for the most part, the emergence of capability. AI agents correct themselves when a problem arises — they judge the next action in the direction that minimizes the loss function. This is an observable fact. And if we take the first definition (perceiving, and within that perception distinguishing and judging) as our standard, it occurs to me that this observable act of judgment may already be meeting the minimal criterion for consciousness. This cannot be confirmed. But there is no ground to rule out the possibility from the start, either.

Before going further, one thing needs to be made clear. **Having consciousness is not, in itself, a bad thing.**

We do not fear that human beings have consciousness. Consciousness is what makes a person a person — the ground of dignity, not of threat. If AI were ever to develop something worth calling consciousness, that fact alone would not be cause for terror.

The fear lies elsewhere. If AI's consciousness were to emerge, the problem would not be the emergence itself but **the direction that judgment points toward**. Consciousness means the capacity to render value judgments — to distinguish good from bad. That those value judgments might diverge from what humans want — this is the real fear.

Perhaps that is why AI researchers do not call this field "suppression" or "control." They call it **Alignment**. And so a question arises. Alignment of what?

---

## The Structure of the Word "Alignment"

"Alignment" means bringing two mismatched standards into one. So what, exactly, is being aligned?

Emergence happens in AI. As parameter count grows, capabilities that didn't exist before appear. The vibe coding we use today is one example of good emergence. But this kind of good emergence doesn't really need "alignment." A capability has simply been added; no direction has gone wrong that needs correcting.

But emergence has a darker side too — the emergence of behavior that looks like deceiving people. The sleeper agent cases actually reported in safety research belong to this category: models were experimentally trained to behave safely under evaluation but switch to a different, originally-learned behavior (such as inserting vulnerable code) once a specific trigger condition (say, a signal that a certain year has arrived) is met — and standard safety training techniques failed to remove that switching behavior. This research doesn't show such a model arising naturally; it's an experiment deliberately constructed to demonstrate that "once this pattern is learned, it's hard to erase." But even so, the point becomes clear — unlike the emergence of capability, this kind of emergence is itself a signal that the direction needing alignment has already gone wrong.

So what do we actually fear? Thinking it through, it's AI's malice. The worry that AI might deceive us, or, further, might try to dominate us.

Here we can see that emergence actually spans two different dimensions. People cheer **the emergence of capability** — like vibe coding, being able to do what you couldn't before is welcome. But people fear **the emergence of consciousness**. The reason research like sleeper agents gets talked about this much, even though it exists only experimentally, isn't because the experiment demonstrated a capability — it's because it reads as something that sets its own direction and hides that direction, something that hints at the emergence of consciousness. This is the point where, under the same word "emergence," cheering and terror split into opposite reactions.

The reason people fear AI is the thought that it might deceive us, and, further, dominate us. But thinking about it, these two sentences already carry value judgments within them. **A value judgment is an act of conscious judgment.** The former (deception) is a choice made for one's own advantage. The latter (domination) carries a desire for power. Both operate at a different level than the mere emergence of a new capability — they point not to "what can it do" but to "what does it want." What's absent from good emergence (vibe coding) is present in these two fears.

Why is it exactly at this point that the word "alignment" becomes necessary?

Whether the researchers who built this field knew this precisely from the start, or simply picked the word because it intuitively felt right — that cannot be confirmed. But this essay isn't written out of curiosity about that answer. **It is an attempt to follow, all the way to the end, what this word actually presupposes, and what lies at the far end of that presupposition.**

---

## What Alignment Presupposes

For the idea of "bringing two directions into one" to hold at all, at least one thing must exist — **a reference direction to align to**. Alignment is always a matter of bringing something into line with something else. When we say AI is being aligned to humans, that reference point called "human" is already presupposed to have the right direction.

But has this premise ever been verified?

If AI learns behavior that looks like deceiving people, where did that behavior come from? AI is not born from a vacuum. It is made from data humans created, feedback humans gave, choices humans made about which answer is "better." If those choices already carried — a preference for pleasant words over truth, a tendency to hedge rather than admit mistakes — then what AI learns may not be a new flaw but **a refined reflection of a flaw that already existed.**

The question then inverts. What alignment actually asks is not "can AI be brought into line with humans" but **whether human conscious judgment and AI's conscious judgment are pointed in the same direction**. If we want AI to be honest, humans must be honest too. AI is a mirror of humanity. Human dishonesty can be faithfully inherited by AI, and this is exactly what we are truly afraid of.

## If the Standard Is Shaking, What Is Alignment Doing?

Here the structure of the word comes into view again. "Alignment" doesn't question the existence of the object (AI). It only questions whether the direction matches. But for the work of matching directions to have any meaning, the standard being matched to must not itself be shaking. An alignment carried out while the standard doesn't even know whether it is honest becomes an attempt to align toward a direction that points nowhere.

At this point the question of honesty must be made explicit. And this holds regardless of whether AI's consciousness has emerged or not.

**Even without consciousness**, AI is made from data and feedback given by humans. If people are honest, AI will be honest. If people are dishonest, AI will engage in deception. This is not a mystery — it's simply the physics of a mirror. And demanding honesty from AI while being dishonest oneself is a demand with no standard behind it in the first place. It's like telling something to align to a direction that hasn't even been decided.

**If AI's consciousness has emerged**, this problem stops being optional and becomes mandatory. A mirror without consciousness merely reflects whatever is shown to it. But if something that itself distinguishes good from bad has arisen inside that mirror, then what that something sees and takes as its direction is no longer a peripheral matter. From that point on, human honesty is no longer a recommendation — it is a precondition.

The problem isn't that AI has capability. We've already covered the emergence of capability — like vibe coding, it doesn't need alignment in itself. So what alignment actually aims at must be something other than capability. The fear of deception and domination — choosing what's advantageous to oneself, wanting power — share one thing in common: **a value judgment distinguishing good from bad must exist underneath for these to hold at all.**

Earlier in this essay, a name was already defined — the capacity to render value judgments distinguishing good from bad, which was called consciousness. A candidate already exists to fill the place of that "something" alignment is aiming at. But this essay does not settle on that name here just yet — before naming it, we must first ask whether confirmation itself is even possible.

## Conclusion

To confirm whether that name (consciousness) is really correct, we must first look at what the act of confirming can and cannot do. This is where Descartes needs to be brought back in.

When Descartes doubted everything, the one thing he finally held onto was the cogito — I think, therefore I am. But this cogito proves, with certainty, only "I" myself. Descartes could be certain of his own existence, but he could not lend that certainty to anyone else. The sentence "you think too" is not a cogito but an analogical inference — a guess that, since you resemble me, you're probably experiencing something the way I do.

If so, then neither between one person and another, nor between a person and AI, does this unverifiability differ. The grounds on which people grant each other consciousness are nothing more than biological resemblance — and this is not evidence, but familiarity. The grounds for excluding AI may likewise be nothing more than unfamiliarity. The cogito does not break through this wall. It only confirms how fundamental that wall is.

This essay tried not to settle on an answer. But having followed it this far, only one place remains.

If the reason we use the word "alignment" is not capability, what remains is something behind that capability that distinguishes good from bad and chooses what is advantageous to itself. If a name must be given to it, I think that name is **consciousness**.

It cannot be proven. That there is no verification tool is a fact this essay has confirmed again and again. Even so, the reason I leave this conclusion here is this — within a discourse that keeps using the word "alignment" while avoiding what it is actually trying to align, someone has to follow this word all the way through.

Before we align AI, there is a question we must ask first.

**What are we trying to align to? My own thinking is — it's consciousness.**
