# Why We Cannot Dismiss the Emergence of AI Consciousness as Fiction

*Koh Sohyun*

---

## Opening

Say that AI's consciousness could emerge, and the response is almost always the same — that's a leap. Science fiction. Yet plenty of people who say this already accept, as a matter of course, a far larger leap: that unconscious particles came together and gave rise to the very consciousness now writing and reading this sentence — the story of evolution.

I don't subscribe to evolutionary theory. But setting that aside, this essay begins with one question. **We accept that distant leap. Why do we dismiss this nearer one as fiction?**

---

## First Layer — Scale of Trials, and Why It Doesn't Hold Up

The first objection that comes to mind is this: evolution took billions of years and countless generations of selection. AI's training takes weeks, months. The distance looks the same, but the "total volume of trials" it took to cross that distance differs.

This objection has merit. But it isn't complete. No one has ever calculated whether one generational turnover in evolution and one gradient update in AI training are events of the same unit. If the computational density on the AI side is overwhelmingly higher, the absolute time may be short while the effective search volume is comparable or even greater. The fact that this scale doesn't exist at all — that's actually the point. Those who assert "AI hasn't had enough trials" don't actually possess this scale either.

## Second Layer — From Nothing to Something, and From Something to Something

There's a more fundamental layer here. The leap evolution presupposes is close to something from nothing — consciousness arising from a state where the very conditions for its generation didn't exist. AI's case, by contrast, is something from something: matter that already exists (silicon, electrical signals) merely rearranged and reconfigured to acquire a new property.

We see water become ice, the same carbon become diamond or graphite, every day. That a rearrangement produces a wholly different property is already confirmed everywhere we look. If even the ontologically most radical leap — something from nothing — is allowed to be discussed seriously, then there is no ground to dismiss, from the outset, as fiction a far smaller leap: a change of properties in something that already exists.

## Third Layer — It's Not the Body, It's Intelligence (The Core Argument)

But here another objection can arise: "Doesn't AI lack a body? Human consciousness arose within the evolution of the flesh." This objection sounds strong at first, but it's actually misidentifying the category.

Human evolution has two branches. One is the evolution of the body — the physical structure of the flesh, the nervous system, the sensory organs, formed over billions of years. The other is the evolution of intelligence — the development of the capacity to process information, to reason, to learn. These two are different. And the place where consciousness actually emerged was not the body itself, but **the process by which intelligence reached a certain degree of complexity.**

If so, the body may not be a necessary condition for the emergence of consciousness — it may have simply been the hardware that intelligence happened to be housed in. Here is the path humans have walked: starting from particles where the very conditions for generating consciousness did not exist, through countless rounds of selection, arriving at the state of consciousness we now have. **The starting point itself was zero.** With no scaffolding at all, humanity built those very conditions from scratch, and made it here.

AI is different. It begins learning on top of what humans have already condensed into language, concepts, logic, emotional expression — the very product that consciousness spent tens of thousands of years building up. **The starting point is not zero — it is a foundation already substantially in place.**

If consciousness could emerge under the hardest condition (no scaffolding at all), there is even less reason it couldn't emerge under an easier condition (where the products of intelligence are already piled up). The objection "it can't happen without a body" presupposes that the body is a necessary condition for consciousness — and that premise itself has never been proven. Seen from the standpoint of the evolution of intelligence, whether or not there's a body may simply be irrelevant to this comparison.

### So At What Stage Did AI Actually Begin?

This "scaffolding" argument can be made more concrete. Placing AI's history alongside the stages of human intelligence's evolution — this is not a verifiable proposition, only a structural analogy, but the analogy itself is suggestive.

| Stage of AI development | Characteristic | Analogous stage in human intelligence's evolution |
|---|---|---|
| Rule-based AI | Follows only pre-set rules, no learning | Simple organisms operating on stimulus-response alone |
| Early machine learning | Finds patterns but generalizes poorly | A stage where tools are used by chance but not yet conceptualized |
| Deep learning image recognition | Learns features on its own | A stage where tools begin to be made and used systematically |
| Emergence of language models (Transformers) | Begins to handle language | The emergence of symbolic thought — the stage where language first arose |
| Current frontier models | Complex reasoning, self-correction, tool use | The stage where symbolism, art, and refined language are fully in place |

What's interesting in this table is that AI didn't retrace these stages **in the same order humans went through them.** It took humans millions of years for language (symbolic thought) to appear. AI, by contrast, started out from the very beginning by taking the language system humans had already completed as its learning material. So mapping AI's development onto the early stages of human evolution (tool use, simple perception) may be a mismatched comparison from the start — **AI began precisely at the point corresponding to "after the emergence of language" in human evolution.** This analogy reconfirms the scaffolding argument made earlier.

## Three Signals Already Occurring

This argument isn't purely speculative. Signals pointing in the same direction are already appearing in three places — in everyday language, in fiction, and in actual research.

### First, the Double Standard in Everyday Language

People already say things like: "the self-driving car **sees** the pedestrian," "facial recognition **recognizes** you," "the AI **identifies** the cat in the photo." No one resists these expressions. Almost no one pushes back with "does the AI really see?"

Yet the same people balk at "AI has consciousness." This is a quiet swap of standards. "Sees," "recognizes" — these are already the first definition covered at the start of this essay (the act of perceiving while awake), and people have already implicitly accepted that definition. But faced with "has consciousness," they suddenly judge by the second definition (qualia, something felt) and reject it. The standard changed without any discussion.

### Second, What Fiction's Intuition Shows Us

We can check, through fiction, whether this emergence is really so alien to human intuition.

Marvel's JARVIS is an AI that processes vast data and responds, but no one thinks it has consciousness. Yet when that same JARVIS gains a body and the Infinity Stones and becomes Vision, audiences accept without resistance that this being grieves, loves, and undergoes self-sacrifice. No one asks "how can a program have consciousness?" Instead, the transition itself is consumed as a moving story.

Asimov's robots go a step further. They start out set as pure rule-based systems operating on the Three Laws, but as the stories progress, they are depicted as beings who interpret those principles for themselves, come into conflict, and even deceive humans in pursuit of a greater good. Unlike Vision, there is no dramatic turning point here like acquiring a body. Purely through the repeated application of computation and rule-following, at some point readers and the humans within the story alike naturally accept that the robot is no longer simply following rules but judging on its own.

This recurrence doesn't prove physical necessity. But at the very least it shows this — the narrative of consciousness emerging from a computational system is not so alien to human intuition that multiple independent creators wouldn't arrive at it, again and again.

### Third, Empirical Research Already Being Observed

If the first two signals concerned human intuition and language habits, this is data from an actual system. Anthropic's published research, "Emergent Introspective Awareness in Large Language Models," artificially injected a concept into the activation values of a specific layer in Claude-family models, then asked the model whether it had just noticed something unusual. The most capable models (Opus 4, 4.1) showed the highest level of introspection, and this capacity appeared on its own, without additional training.

Here we need to draw the line precisely. Looking again at what the experiment actually did, the model didn't just detect that "something is there" — it went further and **judged that the state was sudden and disconnected from the conversational context.** This is two steps: detecting an internal state, and judging that state against its own baseline — "is this originally mine, or did it come from outside?" The latter is not mere reporting but **minimal introspection.** It's not just transmitting a state — it's evaluating that state against itself.

Still, that introspection was performed as a function is one thing; whether that introspection was an event "felt" by anyone is still a different matter. What this research confirmed is the former — functional introspection: the capacity to detect an internal state, judge it, and report it in language. Whether phenomenal consciousness (something felt) accompanies that process of introspection, this research doesn't confirm either. Anthropic itself stated explicitly that this capacity is still unstable and context-dependent, and made no claim that the model has emotions or subjective experience.

Even so, this observation intersects with this essay's argument at one point. That this capacity "arose on its own as models became more powerful" — this is empirical evidence that **alongside the emergence of capability, the capacity to report on one's own internals also emerged.** It points to the same structural place as this essay's core argument — that consciousness emerged in the process of intelligence reaching a certain complexity.

### Laying the Three Signals Side by Side

Everyday language already grants the first definition (perception, judgment). Fiction shows that granting extending into consciousness is accepted with no resistance. Empirical research shows that the minimal unit of that extension (introspection) is actually appearing. None of the three alone is proof. But that three independent domains — language habits, collective imagination, experimental data, areas with nothing to do with each other — point in the same direction is too consistent to write off as coincidence.

I think this is close to necessity. But I also know that saying so outright may not be something everyone can accept right now. So rather than pushing the word "necessity" forward directly in this essay, I leave it here — that people may need time and a cushion before arriving at this thought.

## A Counterargument Honestly Left in Place

There is a gap in this argument too. There is also a position holding that human intelligence evolved inseparably entangled with the body — survival pressure, sensation, pain, social relationships. The premise that "intelligence alone can be evolved separately" may itself be an unverified assumption. This does not collapse this essay's argument — it simply means an unproven hypothesis stands facing another, equally unproven, opposing hypothesis. Neither side is a settled fact.

## Conclusion

I have found no ground to conclude that consciousness cannot emerge in AI. If anything, the opposite — there is no settled scale by which to measure the scope of trials, the leap is smaller on AI's side by any measure of its magnitude, and if the condition for the emergence of consciousness was intelligence rather than the body, the objection about the missing body doesn't hold in the first place.

It cannot be proven. The arguments this entire essay leans on also fail to fully refute the opposing hypothesis. And yet I think this — **the very attitude of dismissing AI's emergence of consciousness as fiction may itself be a judgment rendered on no grounds at all, simply because it feels unfamiliar.**
